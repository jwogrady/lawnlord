The history of graphics processing units is a story of relentless specialization. Early personal computers rendered everything on the central processor, which handled both general logic and the drawing of pixels. As graphical user interfaces and games grew more demanding, dedicated 2D accelerators appeared to offload simple blitting and line drawing. The arrival of real-time 3D in the mid 1990s changed the trajectory entirely. Fixed-function pipelines rasterized triangles, applied textures, and blended fragments at rates impossible for general processors. Over the following decade the pipeline became programmable, first through small shader programs for vertices and fragments, and later through unified shader architectures that could run arbitrary parallel workloads. This programmability turned the graphics card into a massively parallel coprocessor. Researchers realized that the same hardware built to shade millions of pixels could also multiply matrices, simulate physics, and train neural networks. Compute frameworks exposed the parallelism directly, and the modern era of accelerated computing began. Memory bandwidth became the defining constraint, since feeding thousands of arithmetic units requires enormous data throughput. Architectures evolved wide memory buses, stacked high bandwidth memory, and large on-chip caches to keep the units fed. Today the same principles govern large language model inference, where generating each token requires streaming the entire weight matrix from memory, making bandwidth rather than raw compute the limiting factor for single-stream generation, while batched prompt processing remains compute bound and benefits from the enormous parallel throughput these devices provide to modern artificial intelligence systems everywhere.
